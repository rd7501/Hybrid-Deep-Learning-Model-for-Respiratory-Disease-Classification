Model Design
Base Networks: Combined EfficientNet (for lightweight, scalable feature extraction) with DenseNet (for dense connectivity and gradient flow).

Fusion Strategy: Extracted embeddings from both backbones, then concatenated them before passing through fully connected layers.

Classifier Head: Dense layers with dropout regularization, ending in a softmax layer for 4‑class classification (COVID‑19, Pneumonia, TB, Normal).

📊 Dataset
Total samples: 13,209 chest X‑ray images.

Final split:

Training: 8,791

Validation: 2,434

Test: 1,984

Class distribution: Balanced across TB, COVID‑19, Pneumonia, and Normal with optimized class weights to handle residual imbalance.

⚙️ Training & Performance
Optimizer: AdamW with tuned learning rate.

Loss: Categorical cross‑entropy.

Callbacks: EarlyStopping + ModelCheckpoint.

Results:

Training accuracy: ~96%

Validation accuracy: ~95%

Test accuracy: ~95.5%

Test loss: ~0.83

Observations:

COVID vs. Normal → very clean separation.

Pneumonia → widest spread in t‑SNE, reflecting heterogeneous radiographic features.

TB → partial overlap with Pneumonia, consistent with clinical similarities.

🧠 Strengths of Hybrid Approach
EfficientNet brings parameter efficiency and strong generalization.

DenseNet ensures feature reuse and better gradient flow.

Fusion improves robustness compared to single‑backbone models (like plain ResNet50).

t‑SNE visualization confirms improved separability of classes compared to earlier baselines.

🚩 Remaining Gaps
Clinical validation: Needs external hospital datasets to confirm generalization.

Explainability: Integration of Grad‑CAM or saliency maps would strengthen trust.

Multi‑center trials: Required to prove robustness across imaging conditions.
